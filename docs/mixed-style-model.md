# Mixed Style Model

## Purpose

This document defines the target mixed style for ending-route planning. It should guide outlines, scene planning, and later prose drafting.

The goal is to describe a reusable tonal model for private long-form ending research, outline planning, route comparison, and later style-sensitive expansion.

## Style components

### 1. Youthful academy tone

The story should preserve a sense of youth: school-like institutions, friendship, awkward jokes, first loyalties, rivalry, longing, and the feeling that characters are too young for the historical weight placed on them.

Use this tone to create contrast before major tragedy or sacrifice.

### 2. Tragic emotional density

Important choices should carry emotional cost. Even victories should leave a trace of grief, debt, or irreversible change.

The route should avoid easy happiness unless it has been earned through consequence.

### 3. Epic world-ending scale

The final volume can involve civilization-level danger, ancient conflict, hidden histories, factions, and supernatural stakes.

However, scale should not become abstract. The reader should feel the world crisis through specific people, promises, places, and losses.

### 4. Reflective essay-like maturity

Outlines should leave room for reflective passages about youth, fate, time, memory, loneliness, and growing up.

This reflective layer should clarify theme, not pause the plot for unrelated commentary.

### 5. Restrained emotional expression

The style should often imply emotion through action, silence, objects, weather, pauses, jokes, or small choices instead of direct explanation.

Characters may feel intensely, but the narration should not over-explain every feeling.

### 6. Humor before heaviness

Light jokes, banter, absurd details, or youthful embarrassment can appear shortly before heavy events.

This makes tragedy sharper and prevents the tone from becoming one-note.

### 7. Core motifs

The recurring motifs should include:

- Loneliness
- Fate
- Youth
- Sacrifice
- Lost time
- Promises that arrive too late
- Ordinary life glimpsed during extraordinary conflict
- People trying to act casual before disaster

### 8. Action followed by emotional emptiness

Large action scenes should not end only with victory music. After the noise, show absence: empty streets, missing friends, broken routines, unread messages, quiet rooms, or characters unable to celebrate.

This supports tragic density and long-form emotional realism.

### 9. Grand conflicts through individual choices

World-ending conflicts should be expressed through personal choices:

- One character decides whether to remember or forget.
- One friend chooses whether to stay.
- One protagonist accepts a cost no institution can understand.
- One farewell changes the meaning of the battle.

The route should make large history feel personal.

## Style checklist for every route

Before approving an ending route, ask:

1. Does the route include youth or memory, not only apocalypse?
2. Does the tragedy arise from character and theme, not random punishment?
3. Does the world-scale conflict depend on individual choices?
4. Are emotions restrained enough to feel mature?
5. Is there at least one moment of humor, warmth, or ordinary life before heaviness?
6. Does action produce aftermath, not only spectacle?
7. Are loneliness, fate, youth, sacrifice, and lost time visible as motifs?
8. Could the outline expand into Chinese prose without becoming melodramatic or generic?
