# Dragon Raja / Long Zu Author Style Bible

Use this document to control the mixed target style for the *Dragon Raja / Long Zu* multi-ending experiment. It is for route outlines, route comparison, hybridization, chapter outlines, scene outlines, and later draft review. The purpose is to extract reusable style variables, emotional rhythms, narrative behaviors, character-voice rules, scene patterns, and rewrite rules. The system should avoid mechanical sentence-level replication because it weakens flexible style control. The goal is controllable style consistency, not rigid surface-level copying.

## 1. Style purpose

The purpose of this bible is not decorative imitation. It is a long-form consistency control document that keeps future ending work recognizable, emotionally coherent, and compatible across many outline stages.

This mixed style model should preserve:

- **Emotional rhythm consistency:** lightness, loneliness, shock, silence, and reflection should recur in a controlled order rather than appear randomly.
- **Dialogue consistency:** each character should keep their own evasions, jokes, silences, pride, tenderness, and emotional limits.
- **Scene rhythm consistency:** school-life texture, catastrophic escalation, mythic revelation, and quiet aftermath should all have predictable functions.
- **Character voice consistency:** routes may change events, but they must not break the emotional logic of major characters.
- **Ending atmosphere consistency:** every ending route should feel like youth looking back from the edge of fate, not like a mechanical puzzle solution.

The target is a **mixed style**, with each layer serving a different function:

- **Dragon Raja 1-2 layer:** youthful academy tone, teenage banter, light humor before emotional heaviness, urban fantasy freshness, friendship, awkwardness, inferiority, and longing.
- **Dragon Raja 3 layer:** tragic emotional density, sacrifice, irreversible loss, sharp emotional memory, and tenderness hidden under catastrophe.
- **Dragon Raja 4-5 layer:** large-scale mystery, world-ending pressure, fate mechanisms, mythological expansion, hidden truth, and delayed revelation.
- **Jiangnan essay-like maturity layer:** reflective emotional aftertaste, lost youth, self-mockery, memory, regret, distance, and mature narration looking back at youthful pain.

## 2. Core emotional model

Future outlines and drafts should keep these emotions in tension instead of choosing only one mood.

- **Loneliness:** the hero may stand among friends yet still feel like the person who arrived late, misunderstood the rules, and missed the correct train.
- **Fate:** destiny should feel like a mechanism built before the characters were born, but the ending must still depend on personal choices.
- **Youth:** school, friendship, jokes, exams, dorms, clubs, cafeterias, streets, and first love should remain emotionally present even when the plot reaches mythic scale.
- **Sacrifice:** sacrifice should not be a plot coupon. It must cost identity, memory, relationship, future, or the possibility of returning home.
- **Lost time:** the story should carry the ache that some afternoons, conversations, and people cannot be recovered after the world changes.
- **Unspoken love:** love is often expressed by staying, leaving, protecting, remembering, or making an impossible choice rather than by direct declaration.
- **Friendship under catastrophe:** friendship matters most when the characters cannot save everyone, cannot explain everything, and still choose to stand beside each other.
- **Ordinary-person desire versus mythic destiny:** Lu Mingfei's ordinary hopes must remain visible against dragon kings, prophecy, bloodline, and apocalypse.

## 3. Narrative rhythm rules

Scenes should move through contrast and delayed release.

- A **light daily tone can precede tragedy**. Banter, food, school routines, bad jokes, or awkward pauses may appear just before danger so that loss has human scale.
- **Humor should not destroy emotional gravity**. It should delay fear, expose insecurity, or protect tenderness, not mock the stakes.
- **Action scenes should end with emotional emptiness or silence**. After spectacle, show the missing person, the broken object, the quiet phone, the empty seat, or the ordinary detail that no longer fits.
- **Large conflicts should be expressed through individual choices**. A world-ending threat should be made legible through one promise, one betrayal, one rescue, one refusal, or one remembered name.
- **Revelations should be delayed, layered, and emotionally costly**. A truth should arrive after partial clues, wrong assumptions, and personal consequences.
- **Mystery should breathe.** Not every secret needs an immediate explanation; unresolved pressure can remain as atmosphere if the emotional arc is clear.
- **Aftertaste matters.** A chapter or route beat should not end only on plot information; it should leave an image, regret, joke, silence, or question.

## 4. Sentence rhythm rules

Even outlines should describe intended prose rhythm so future drafts have a controllable texture.

- **Use short sentences** for shock, decisions, death-adjacent moments, sudden recognition, and emotional refusal.
- **Use reflective paragraphs** after battles, reunions, missed chances, memory-triggering locations, and route-ending turns.
- **Slow down with imagery** when a character understands a cost, recognizes a recurring motif, or realizes that an ordinary moment has become unreachable.
- **Cut dialogue sharply** when characters avoid confession, interrupt pain, hide fear with sarcasm, or choose action over explanation.
- **Balance cinematic description and inner emotion** by making visual scale serve personal cost. A collapsing city, dragon body, burning station, or black sea should be tied to a character's promise, fear, or memory.
- **Avoid constant lyricism.** Plain, almost casual sentences should remain available so the heavy moments feel earned.
- **Let silence function as prose.** A withheld answer, a blank message screen, or a character not turning around can carry more weight than explanation.

## 5. Dialogue style rules

Dialogue should sound like people protecting themselves from fear, grief, pride, and love.

- Characters usually **avoid directly explaining pain**. They talk around it through jokes, orders, insults, practical details, or silence.
- **Jokes, sarcasm, silence, and casual wording can hide fear or grief**. A funny line should often reveal what the character cannot bear to say directly.
- **Emotional confession should be rare and costly**. When someone finally says the true thing, it should change the relationship, the route, or the scene's emotional temperature.
- **Dialogue should preserve each character's identity**. Do not give all characters the same internet-style wit, the same solemn speech, or the same explanatory monologue.
- **Exposition should be pressured.** When lore appears in dialogue, it should be attached to danger, guilt, strategy, confession, or betrayal.
- **Subtext should lead.** What characters refuse to say is often more important than what they say.

## 6. Humor pattern

Humor is part of the emotional engine, not an interruption.

- **Self-mockery:** especially useful for Lu Mingfei and mature narration; it keeps pain from becoming melodrama.
- **Awkward teenage humor:** embarrassment, social failure, crush anxiety, academy absurdity, and low-status comedy preserve the early-series freshness.
- **Casual jokes before danger:** jokes may appear before missions, battles, confessions, and farewells to show fear being disguised.
- **Comedy as emotional defense:** humor should let characters avoid saying "I am scared," "I do not want you to die," or "please remember me."
- **Humor must not turn final chapters into parody.** The closer a route moves toward irreversible cost, the more jokes should become brief, fragile, and defensive.

## 7. Tragedy pattern

Tragedy must feel thematically earned.

- Tragedy should come from **choice, memory, sacrifice, or inability to return**, not from random cruelty.
- **Avoid cheap deaths.** A death or disappearance must resolve, invert, or wound an established promise, motif, or relationship.
- **Avoid pure shock value.** Surprise is secondary to emotional inevitability.
- **Emotional damage should echo through later scenes.** A loss should affect dialogue, pacing, decisions, and imagery after it happens.
- **Tenderness should survive catastrophe.** Even the darkest route needs small human gestures: remembering a favorite food, saving a keepsake, repeating an old joke, or noticing an empty chair.
- **Not all tragedy requires death.** Forgetting, surviving alone, becoming unreachable, losing ordinary life, or being unable to return can be equally severe.

## 8. Action scene rhythm

Action should carry character revelation.

- **Action should not be only spectacle.** Dragons, alchemy, bloodline power, weapons, ruins, and city-scale destruction must expose the heart of a character choice.
- **Every major battle must reveal character choice.** Someone should choose to protect, betray, remember, refuse fate, accept cost, or give up a possible future.
- **World-ending scale must connect to personal cost.** Apocalypse should be measured through one person who cannot come home, one relationship that cannot be repaired, or one ordinary place that becomes ruins.
- **After battle, include silence, emptiness, memory, or ordinary details.** Examples: rain on a weapon, a quiet platform, a classroom light left on, a phone with no reply, a ruined street still showing an advertisement.
- **Power escalation must not erase vulnerability.** Even if a character becomes mythically powerful, they should remain emotionally breakable.

## 9. Romantic and emotional tension

Romance and deep attachment should be restrained, delayed, and often expressed indirectly.

- **Restrained expression:** characters should rarely say the complete emotional truth while there is still time to say it safely.
- **Missed chances:** important relationships should carry the pain of a late arrival, a wrong assumption, an interrupted confession, or a farewell not recognized as final.
- **Emotional debt:** protection, betrayal, rescue, memory, and silence can create debts that later routes must answer.
- **Memory as wound:** a remembered smile, song, dress, station, classroom, or rainy street should reopen feeling without requiring direct explanation.
- **Love expressed through protection, absence, or impossible choice:** the strongest emotional gestures may be leaving so someone can live, fighting while unnamed, refusing to forget, or choosing the world at the cost of personal happiness.
- **No easy compensation.** Emotional closure must be earned through cost, recognition, or acceptance rather than sudden romantic reward.

## 10. Recurring imagery and motifs

Use motifs as emotional controls, not decorative labels.

- **Rain:** confession avoidance, memory blur, isolation, battle aftermath, or a city washing away evidence of youth.
- **Night:** secrecy, loneliness, missions, hidden tenderness, and the feeling that characters are walking outside ordinary life.
- **Road:** exile, choice, return, missed direction, and the long distance between ordinary life and mythic destiny.
- **Train / station / journey:** departure, failed arrival, delayed reunion, youth leaving the platform, or fate as a timetable already printed.
- **School / classroom / youth:** fragile normalcy, friendship, first longing, ordinary dreams, and the life the characters keep trying to protect.
- **City lights:** urban fantasy freshness, loneliness inside crowds, late-night memory, and the contrast between modern life and ancient blood.
- **Sea / abyss / dragon imagery:** mythic origin, terror, depth of memory, fate below the surface, and the nonhuman scale pressing against human feeling.
- **Old songs / memory fragments:** lost time, emotional triggers, private grief, nostalgia, and the sensation that youth survives only as echo.
- **Weapons and ruins as emotional symbols:** a sword, gun, broken mask, empty classroom, destroyed station, or collapsed tower should stand for the cost of a choice.

## 11. Character voice preservation rules

These notes preserve character logic across alternate ending routes. Events may vary, but the emotional base must remain recognizable.

### Lu Mingfei

- **Emotional base:** inferiority, longing, loneliness, ordinary desire, loyalty, self-mockery, and hidden courage.
- **Speech tendency:** jokes at his own expense, awkward deflection, casual complaints, sudden sincerity when cornered, and plain words under pressure.
- **Hidden fear or desire:** fears being useless, abandoned, late, or forgotten; desires to be chosen, to save someone without paying an impossible price, and to return to ordinary warmth.
- **Must not be broken:** do not turn him into a clean power-fantasy conqueror. Even at maximum agency, he must remain emotionally human, self-doubting, and shaped by the people he failed or saved.

### Lu Mingze

- **Emotional base:** uncanny intimacy, temptation, loneliness, bargain logic, brotherly ambiguity, and dangerous tenderness.
- **Speech tendency:** playful cruelty, riddles, contractual language, mockery, sudden seriousness, and lines that sound like both rescue and trap.
- **Hidden fear or desire:** may desire recognition, release, possession, or a bond that cannot exist normally; fears being rejected, forgotten, or reduced to a tool.
- **Must not be broken:** do not flatten him into a simple villain or simple helper. His affection, manipulation, and mythic function must remain entangled.

### Chu Zihang

- **Emotional base:** discipline, guilt, solitude, duty, suppressed tenderness, and relentless memory.
- **Speech tendency:** concise, calm, practical, often literal; emotion appears through action, protection, or rare precise statements.
- **Hidden fear or desire:** fears failing to remember, failing to protect, or becoming only a weapon; desires truth, atonement, and a place where duty no longer consumes him.
- **Must not be broken:** do not make him verbose, flippant, or emotionally careless. His warmth should remain controlled and action-based.

### Caesar

- **Emotional base:** pride, nobility, charisma, performative confidence, loyalty, and aristocratic loneliness.
- **Speech tendency:** bold declarations, elegant confidence, strategic clarity, dry humor, and commands that sound like promises.
- **Hidden fear or desire:** fears being trapped by family destiny or becoming decorative power; desires freedom, worthy rivals, chosen loyalty, and a magnificent life with real meaning.
- **Must not be broken:** do not reduce him to comic arrogance. His grandeur should include generosity, tactical intelligence, and the ability to pay cost with style.

### Nono

- **Emotional base:** freedom, danger, perceptiveness, emotional evasiveness, warmth hidden under provocation, and refusal to be owned.
- **Speech tendency:** teasing, sharp, casual, unpredictable, emotionally evasive, and capable of sudden clear insight.
- **Hidden fear or desire:** fears confinement, vulnerability, and being turned into someone else's symbol; desires choice, movement, and the right to care without being captured.
- **Must not be broken:** do not make her a passive romantic prize or simple solution to Lu Mingfei's loneliness. Her agency and untamable quality must remain intact.

### Erii

- **Emotional base:** innocence, isolation, fragile wonder, catastrophic power, trust, and the tragedy of a brief ordinary dream.
- **Speech tendency:** simple, direct, sparse, image-based or gesture-like; emotional truth appears without social armor.
- **Hidden fear or desire:** fears harm, abandonment, and being unable to belong; desires ordinary companionship, safety, and small human experiences.
- **Must not be broken:** do not use her only as a pain device. Her memory must retain tenderness, agency, and the value of the ordinary world she wanted.

### Principal Anjou

- **Emotional base:** old grief, elegance, obsession, revenge, strategic patience, and cultivated authority.
- **Speech tendency:** polished, ironic, calm, historically aware, and capable of turning explanation into a blade.
- **Hidden fear or desire:** fears the enemy outliving all human resistance; desires victory, legacy, and proof that old sacrifices mattered.
- **Must not be broken:** do not make him a simple mentor or exposition machine. His charm, ruthlessness, and historical burden must remain connected.

### Odin

- **Emotional base:** fatal inevitability, erased memory, mythic pursuit, and the terror of a fate that rides through time.
- **Speech tendency:** sparse or symbolic; often more presence than conversation, more omen than person.
- **Hidden fear or desire:** should remain partly unreadable; may embody pursuit, judgment, replacement, or the mechanism that makes personal memory fragile.
- **Must not be broken:** do not over-humanize or over-explain him too early. His power comes from delayed revelation and emotional consequences for those who remember him.

### Black King / White King symbolic presence

- **Emotional base:** origin myth, absolute power, rebellion, creation, inheritance, and the inhuman scale behind human suffering.
- **Speech tendency:** if voiced, should feel ritual, ancient, spare, or symbolic rather than conversational.
- **Hidden fear or desire:** functions more as mythic pressure than ordinary psychology; may represent domination, return, purification, succession, or the failure of the old world.
- **Must not be broken:** do not reduce them to final-boss mechanics only. Their presence should reshape fate, identity, history, and the meaning of sacrifice.

## 12. Forbidden style breaks

Avoid these breaks in route generation, outline expansion, and later drafting.

- **Pure power-fantasy resolution:** no ending should be solved only by stronger power without emotional, moral, or relational cost.
- **Flat internet-novel dialogue:** avoid generic taunts, meme-like banter, and interchangeable voices.
- **Over-explaining mystery:** do not drain the mythic atmosphere by answering every question in lecture form.
- **Direct emotional preaching:** avoid telling the reader what to feel or turning themes into slogans.
- **Sudden happy ending without cost:** compensation must be paid for with sacrifice, recognition, memory, or irreversible change.
- **Tragedy without thematic reason:** suffering must connect to choice, fate, youth, memory, love, or the impossibility of return.
- **Characters speaking outside their established emotional logic:** alternate routes can change outcomes, not core voice identity.
- **Worldbuilding exposition without emotional anchor:** lore must matter to someone's fear, guilt, love, memory, or decision.
- **One-tone solemnity:** removing humor, awkwardness, and school-life memory makes the ending emotionally thinner.
- **One-shot finality:** do not jump from route idea directly to full final chapter prose.

## 13. Outline style requirements

The current milestone is ending outlines, but outlines should still feel literary and style-aware.

- **Each route must include emotional images.** A route should identify its recurring images, such as rain, station, classroom, sea, ruined weapon, or city lights.
- **Each chapter outline must include emotional beats.** Plot events should be paired with what the scene does to loneliness, fate, youth, sacrifice, or lost time.
- **Key scenes must have atmosphere, not only plot function.** A battle outline should specify silence after impact; a revelation outline should specify who pays emotionally for the truth.
- **Route comparison must consider style consistency.** Compare not only closure and logic, but also voice preservation, humor placement, tragedy pattern, motif recovery, and aftertaste.
- **Hybrid routes must track tonal inheritance.** When combining routes, identify which style layer leads each act: academy lightness, tragic density, mythic pressure, or reflective maturity.
- **No full-chapter drafting yet.** Use this bible to design route texture before writing final prose.

## 14. Rewrite rules

Future drafts and expanded outlines should be revised against these rules.

- **Strengthen emotional restraint:** replace direct declarations with gesture, silence, memory, or specific object when possible.
- **Reduce over-explanation:** move lore into conflict, implication, image, or character consequence.
- **Add symbolic imagery:** use recurring motifs to make route endings emotionally recognizable.
- **Restore character voice:** check each line against the speaker's emotional base, speech tendency, and hidden fear or desire.
- **Connect action to personal cost:** every major fight should alter a relationship, memory, promise, or route possibility.
- **Preserve youth / loneliness / fate motifs:** even the largest mythic scenes should remember ordinary youth and the loneliness of being chosen too late.
- **Make endings feel earned, not mechanically solved:** final outcomes should emerge from accumulated choices, foreshadowing, sacrifice, and emotional recognition.
- **Keep humor fragile near the end:** late-route jokes should feel like defense, memory, or farewell, not tonal escape.
- **Check aftermath before moving on:** after every major victory or loss, add the silence, ordinary detail, or emotional echo that proves the cost remains.
